A technical SEO audit is a structured examination of the factors that determine whether search engines can **find, crawl, index, and rank** your site. Think of it as an MOT for your website.

## What does a technical audit cover?

### Crawlability and indexation
Before Google can rank a page, it must be able to reach and read it. Auditors check `robots.txt`, XML sitemaps, canonical tags, and crawl logs to identify orphaned pages, blocked resources, or misconfigured directives that silently exclude content from the index.

### Core Web Vitals
Google's page experience signals — Largest Contentful Paint (LCP), Interaction to Next Paint (INP), and Cumulative Layout Shift (CLS) — directly influence rankings. An audit measures these in the field (via CrUX data) and in the lab, then maps fixes to their expected ranking impact.

### Site architecture and internal linking
A flat, logical site architecture distributes PageRank efficiently and gives users short paths to any content. Auditors map the link graph, flag deep pages, and identify orphaned content that receives no internal links.

### Structured data and schema markup
Rich results (FAQs, how-tos, review stars, breadcrumbs) increase click-through rate without moving rankings. An audit checks existing schema for errors and spots missed opportunities across page templates.

### HTTPS, redirects, and canonicals
Duplicate content, redirect chains, and mixed-content warnings are audit staples. Left unresolved they dilute PageRank and confuse crawl budget.

## What you get at the end

A good technical audit delivers:
- A prioritised issue list with estimated ranking impact
- Step-by-step remediation instructions developers can act on immediately
- Benchmark metrics so you can measure improvement over time

## How often should you audit?

For most sites, a full technical audit once a year — plus lightweight monthly crawls — is sufficient. After a major redesign or migration, audit immediately before and immediately after launch.
